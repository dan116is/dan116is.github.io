## STEP 2 — THE "SO WHAT" TEST

**Rule:** If any phrase could describe a competitor without changing a word — rewrite it.

Go phrase by phrase and ask:

| Phrase from your statement | Could a competitor say this? | Rewrite if YES |
|---|---|---|
| Your target customer | Yes / No | |
| Their problem/desire | Yes / No | |
| Your category | Yes / No | |
| Your key benefit | Yes / No | |
| Your differentiator | Yes / No | |

**Red flag phrases to eliminate:**
- "better results" → better than what, for whom, measured how?
- "easy to use" → everyone says this
- "saves time" → prove it with a number or mechanism
- "all-in-one" → vague unless you name the specific jobs it replaces
- "trusted by teams" → says nothing specific

**After each rewrite, ask:**
> "Could my #1 competitor put their logo on this sentence and send it out tomorrow?"

If yes — keep rewriting.

**Headline (under 10 words):** The outcome, not the product.
**Subheadline (1–2 sentences):** The mechanism and who it's for.

## STEP 5 — THE ONE SENTENCE VERSION

**Rule:** Under 15 words. Must still pass the So What test.

## STEP 6 — VALIDATION QUESTION

**Goal:** One question to ask 5 potential customers *before* you commit to this positioning.

**The best validation question:**
- Is open-ended (not yes/no)
- Reveals whether they feel the problem the way you framed it
- Does not reveal your solution or lead the witness

**Formula:**
> "When you think about [the problem space you operate in], what's the most frustrating part of how most [companies / tools / approaches] handle it today?"

Their answer tells you:
- Whether your framing matches their lived experience
- Which words they actually use (steal them for your copy)
- Whether your differentiator is something they'd pay to solve

```
Your validation question:
______________________________________________________________
______________________________________________________________

What you're listening for:
______________________________________________________________
```

`[EXAMPLE]`
> **Question:** "When you think about figuring out your market positioning, what's the most frustrating part of how most approaches — workshops, consultants, DIY — handle it today?"
>
> **Listening for:** Do they mention speed? Subjectivity? Team alignment? Buyer perspective?
> If they name the same pain your differentiator solves → positioning is real.
> If they name something else → revise before committing.

## POSITIONING AUDIT CHECKLIST

Before you ship this positioning anywhere, verify:

- [ ] The target customer is a specific person, not a market segment
- [ ] The problem is something they feel, not something you observe
- [ ] The category is a frame of reference your buyer already understands
- [ ] The benefit is a specific outcome, not a feature or capability
- [ ] The differentiator is something you can defend and competitors cannot easily copy
- [ ] Every phrase fails if you replace your name with a competitor's name
- [ ] A stranger could read the one-sentence version and know exactly who it's for
- [ ] You have tested the validation question with at least 5 real humans

## REFERENCE — APRIL DUNFORD'S 5 COMPONENTS OF POSITIONING

| Component | Question it answers |
|---|---|
| **Competitive alternatives** | What would customers do if you didn't exist? |
| **Unique attributes** | What do you have that alternatives do not? |
| **Value** | So what? Why do those attributes matter? |
| **Target customer** | Who cares most about that value? |
| **Market frame of reference** | What context makes your value obvious? |

Source: *Obviously Awesome* by April Dunford (2019)
